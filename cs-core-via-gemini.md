## üß† I. Core Mathematical Skills for CS Research

CS research, particularly in advanced fields, demands fluency in applying these mathematical concepts. Proficiency means being able to **formally prove, analyze, and justify** computational models and results.

| Mathematical Area | Research Relevance (CS Focus) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Discrete Mathematics & Logic** | **Formal Proof Techniques** (Induction, Contradiction), **Graph Theory**, and **Set Theory**. Essential for: **Algorithm analysis** ($O()$ complexity), proving security protocols, and formal verification of systems. |
| **Probability Theory & Statistics** | **Random Variables**, **Bayes' Theorem**, **Hypothesis Testing** (t-tests, ANOVA), and **Confidence Intervals**. Critical for: **Machine Learning model evaluation**, data sampling in large-scale systems, and judging the **statistical significance** of experimental results. |
| **Linear Algebra** | **Vector Spaces**, **Matrix Factorization** (SVD, PCA), **Eigenvalues**, and **Transformations**. The foundational language for: **Machine Learning** (neural network weights), **Computer Graphics**, and optimization problems. |
| **Calculus & Optimization** | **Multivariate Calculus** (Partial Derivatives, Chain Rule), **Gradient Descent**, and **Lagrange Multipliers**. Crucial for: **Designing optimization algorithms** (e.g., backpropagation) and understanding training dynamics in AI. |

### 1. **Rigor & Reproducibility**
| Skill Area | Rationale for Research & Review |

| Skill Area | Rationale for Research & Review |

## üó∫Ô∏è III. CS Junior Research & Peer Review Roadmap Checklist

This checklist provides concrete, prioritized steps for your Junior year to maximize research involvement and prepare a top-tier graduate school application.

### **Phase 1: Foundation Building (Junior Year - Fall/Winter)**

* [ ] **Identify a CS Sub-Field:** Select **1-2 specific research areas** (e.g., Natural Language Processing, Computer Architecture, Distributed Systems).
* [ ] **Secure a Faculty Mentor:** Send **personalized emails** to 3-5 professors whose work aligns with your sub-field. Ask to work on a **well-defined, small starter project** (e.g., replicating an existing paper).
* [ ] **Deep-Dive Literature Review:** Read and take detailed notes on the **10 most-cited papers** from your mentor's lab/sub-field. Critically focus on their **Limitations and Future Work** sections to find new problems.
* [ ] **Set up LaTeX:** Create an Overleaf account and write a **3-page technical summary or literature review** using a standard ACM/IEEE conference template.
* [ ] **Reinforce Math:** Review/Self-study key application topics from **Probability** and **Linear Algebra** relevant to your specific sub-field.

### **Phase 2: Execution & Validation (Junior Year - Spring/Summer)**

* [ ] **Complete First Research Project:** Finish a small, self-contained project with clear, measurable results. This should involve **designing a method** and **evaluating it rigorously**.
* [ ] **Apply for REUs/Summer Internships:** Aggressively target **NSF Research Experiences for Undergraduates (REU)** programs or research internships at tech companies/national labs.
* [ ] **Ensure Reproducibility:** Document your project thoroughly. Final code should be on **GitHub**, include a clear `README.md`, and the **experimental environment** (via Docker or Conda) must be documented.
* [ ] **Draft a Technical Report:** Write a full **8-10 page technical report** in LaTeX following the **IMRAD structure** based on your completed project.

### **Phase 3: Contribution & Peer Review (Senior Year - Fall)**

* [ ] **Submit to a Student Competition:** Submit your research work to the **ACM Student Research Competition (SRC)** or a relevant local/regional workshop.
* [ ] **Seek Co-Review Opportunity (Crucial):** Ask your faculty mentor if you can formally **co-review a paper** with them for a major conference (e.g., ICML, SIGGRAPH, SOSP). Write your own comprehensive review draft first.
* [ ] **Draft Research Statement:** Write a strong **Statement of Purpose (SOP)** that explicitly connects your research contributions (methods, results, and technical skills) to the research goals of the graduate programs you are applying to.
* [ ] **Obtain Research-Focused LORs:** Secure **at least two letters of recommendation** from professors who can speak specifically to your **research skills, analytical rigor, and project independence**.
